The **Defense Informatization Cartel (국방정보화 카르텔, DIC)** is the term used in the book *Beyond Cybersecurity* to describe the collective of defense-sector agencies that allegedly conspired to cover up the root cause of the 2016 Defense Integrated Data Center (DIDC) North Korean hacking incident. The cartel is the organizational substrate through which all 7 layers of the [[7-layer-proof-system|proof system]] operate — each member agency performs a specific function in a distributed attack geometry (see [[apt-style-individual-targeting|APT-Style Individual Targeting]]).

## Conspiracy mechanisms

1. **Division of roles** — each agency handles only a specific phase, diluting individual responsibility across the organizational boundary.
2. **Information monopoly** — selective control of critical information to block external oversight.
3. **Power maintenance** — preventing massive accountability if the 15-year cybersecurity violations were exposed.
4. **Groupthink** — suppression of dissent within member organizations.

## Motive for cover-up

The book argues that if old KIATIS's 15-year Internet exposure (2007–2022) without VPN were publicly revealed:

- The root cause of the 2016 DIDC hacking would be traced to **internal** security failures, not external North Korean sophistication alone.
- Massive accountability for long-term regulatory violations across multiple agencies would follow.
- Public trust in the entire defense cybersecurity system would collapse.

The cartel's cover-up is framed as **power preservation** rather than individual malice.

## Key Takeaways

- [진리성] The cartel is not a single conspiratorial organization but a **distributed attack geometry** across multiple defense-sector agencies, each performing one function.
- [진리성] The cartel's alleged motive is institutional self-preservation: avoiding accountability for 15 years of undisclosed cybersecurity regulation violations.
- [타당성] The three-tier organizational structure assigned by [[../regulations/defense-it-2129-article-11|훈령 제2129호 제11조]] (사업통제 / 주관 / 관리기관) maps directly to three of the cartel member agencies for the KIATIS project. This regulatory designation provides the legal scaffolding through which the alleged concealment operated.
- [진실성] The distributed nature of the cartel is what makes legal attribution difficult: no single individual visibly commits the entire offense, which the book argues requires a new legal framework beyond conventional conspiracy doctrine.
- [진리성] The book's argument that KIDA allegedly **fabricated** academic justification for DT&E/OT&E integration (by misrepresenting the US DoD OT&E for Information and Business Systems regulation) is a directly falsifiable claim pending ingest of that US regulation for textual comparison.
